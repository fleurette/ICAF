                                    “My Special Place”—it’s a magical phrase, a few short words 
that can open a window into a world of wonders. Miss Mary 
(Mary Scuka) of Young Masters Art Center school for visual arts 
in Kensington, Marlyand used these words to spark creative 
talents in her children’s art workshop at the World Children’s 
Festival (WCF) on July 30 and 31 on the National Mall. Your 
special place, Mary explained, “is a place that you have in your 
very own imagination. It’s a place that’s just for you, where you 
feel safe, where you can play and be yourself.”
Mary and her business partner, Miss Carol (Carol Vogel), are the 
founders and principal instructors of Young Masters Art Center 
(YMAC), an in-person and virtual art school that teaches children 
ages four to sixteen in fine arts, drawing, and painting.  The WCF 
is a quadrennial celebration of children’s creativity produced by 
the International Child Art Foundation (ICAF).  Mary and Carol 
created the My Special Place workshop to support the WCF 
themes of imagination and creativity on July 30 and peace and 
leadership on July 31.
Each day of the workshop children donned disposable smocks 
and listened eagerly as Mary and Carol explained the topic of 
My Special Place, set out an individual canvas for each, and 
distributed art mediums and painting tools.  First, there was an 
artist’s pencil and eraser to draw the painting.  “We teach children 
to draw their composition first,” says Mary.  “That makes it much 
easier to create the composition and design, and then to paint.”
Some children pictured their special place right away— “A magic 
carriage!” “An ocean with stars!” “A garden full of flowers!”—
while others needed gentle questioning from Mary and Carol 
to connect with their imaginations and explore their ideas.  “Is 
it outside or inside?”  “Is it in the mountains, maybe at the 
beach?”  “Is anyone there with you?”  Mary illustrated with her 
own special place painting, a colorful treehouse nestled in vines 
at the end of a curving path.  Carol used a giant sketchpad to 
show students how to create their special places from simple 
shapes like circles, ovals, and squares.
Once the students laid in their drawings, it was time to paint. 
 
Mary and Carol helped children choose their colors—purple and 
red flip-flops on a beach, gold for a sun rising over mountain 
peaks, bold blue for a woodland sky—and handed out the paints. 
 
For the youngest children, there were easy-to-grasp tempera 
paint sticks, for the older ones, acrylic paints and an array of paint 
brushes.  Mary and Carol showed the children how a flat-tipped 
brush, a pointed brush, a small tip, or a larger tip can work best 
on their paintings.  “We like to show our students how the right 
tools make a difference,” Mary says with a smile.
YMAC received ICAF’s invitation to conduct the festival workshops 
after its students competed successfully in the 6th Arts Olympiad, 
a worldwide child art contest conducted quadrennially by ICAF 
as the “Olympics” of children’s imagination. YMAC’s teaching 
methods and philosophy align closely with ICAF’s mission and 
principles.  ICAF seeks to raise academic performance and foster 
global citizenship by nurturing creativity and empathy through 
the arts.  YMAC believes, in Mary’s words, that “anyone of any 
age can learn to draw and paint with the right instruction.”  YMAC 
combines creative inspiration—like “My Special Place”—with 
systematic drawing instruction and lots of individual attention to 
help students develop their art skills and an empathic outlook to 
create exciting art.  Both ICAF and YMAC seek to lift students 
over the “fourth-grade slump” which, research shows, starts to 
dampen creativity in children ages nine to thirteen.
YMAC’s workshops at the festival were open to children of any 
age.  The young artists on the Mall ranged in age from two to 
fifteen, and the works they produced were as varied as their 
ages.  There were mountains, forests, oceans, a construction 
site full of vehicles, a jungle with a friendly tiger, a shy giraffe. 
 
One child zoomed in on starfish from the ocean; another zoomed 
out to show the whole blue-and-green earth.  Others created 
abstract works bursting with movement and color.  Thanks to 
ICAF’s invitation and the skilled teaching of Mary and Carol, 
students at the WCF arrived as curious children and emerged 
as Young Art Masters, expressing imagination and empathy 
through joyful works of art.
